A function \(f\) of two independent variables \(x\) and \(y\) has two first order partial derivatives, \(f_x=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\) and \(f_y=\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\text{.}\) As we saw in Preview ActivityÂ 11.4.1, each of these first-order partial derivatives has two partial derivatives, giving a total of four second-order partial derivatives:
  • \(\displaystyle f_{xx} = (f_x)_x = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Bigl[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \Bigr] = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\)
  • \(\displaystyle f_{yy} = (f_y)_y=\frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Bigl[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \Bigr] = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}\)
  • \(\displaystyle f_{xy} = (f_x)_y=\frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Bigl[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \Bigr] = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x}\)
  • \(\displaystyle f_{yx}=(f_y)_x=\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Bigl[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \Bigr] = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}\)
