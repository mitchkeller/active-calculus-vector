    Section 10.6 Splitting the Acceleration Vector

    Motivating Questions

    • How can we split the acceleration vector into parts in the direction of travel and the direction of turning?
    Position is the central measurement of kinematics, the study of motion. Velocity and acceleration are the first and second derivatives of position, respectively and often serve as vital properties in the study of physics and mechanics. To better understand velocity as a vector valued function of time, we examined how the magnitude and direction of position change separately. In particular, we separated velocity into its critical parts, namely speed (magnitude of velocity) and unit tangent vector (direction of travel). Speed is a scalar measurement for how fast the position of an object is changing as a function of time and \(\vT\) is a unit vector that measures in what direction position is changing.
    Acceleration (as a vector quantity) is related to many important physical laws and descriptions. For example, Newtons Second Law of Motion relates the forces acting on an object to its acceleration through the formula \(\vec{F}_{net}= m \vec{a}\text{.}\) This is often summarized as the sum of the forces acting on an object is the mass times the acceleration of the object. This description relates a combination of forces on a object to the position and velocity (through integration of \(\va\)). For a basic example of these calculations look at Example10.2.12.
    In order to understand acceleration better as a vector quantity, we will examine acceleration as a change in the magnitude and direction of the velocity vector. In this section, we will use our knowledge of calculus and vector measurements to split the acceleration vector into parts related to the changes in the magnitude and direction of the velocity vector. Further, we will make sense of these different measurements as they relate to physical contexts.

In this section, we will look at how to calculate these separate parts algebraically, understand what is being measured by these quantities, and show how these tools will give us important physical measurements as well as an efficient tool for calculating \(\vN\text{,}\) the unit normal vector to a curve.

    Subsection 10.6.1 Splitting Acceleration

    Recall that by the definition of \(\vT\), \(\vv\) is parallel to \(\vT\) (when \(\vv\) is not the zero vector or DNE) and as a consequence of how \(\vT\) and \(\vN\) are defined, \(\vT \cdot \vN =0 \text{.}\) In other words, ALL of the velocity is in a direction parallel to the unit tangent vector and the unit normal is always orthogonal to the unit tangent to a curve (when they exist). In this subsection, we will look at splitting the acceleration vector into parts in the direction of travel and the direction of turning.
    For right now, lets suppose that we have the acceleration vector, \(\va(t)\text{,}\) for the motion along some curve in \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\) From Section10.4, we have the TNB frame as a way of describing key directions related to motion along a curve in space. We would like to split the acceleration vector up into parts in each of these directions (\(\vT\text{,}\) \(\vN\text{,}\) and \(\vB\)). In particular, we want to find \(c_1\text{,}\) \(c_2\text{,}\) and \(c_3\) such that \(\va= c_1 \vT + c_2 \vN +c_3 \vB\text{.}\) Remember that \(\vT \cdot \vN =0\text{,}\) \(\vT \cdot \vB =0\text{,}\) \(\vN \cdot \vB =0\text{,}\) and \(\vecmag{\vT}=\vecmag{\vN}=\vecmag{\vB}=1\text{.}\) We can take the dot product of each of \(\vT\text{,}\) \(\vN\text{,}\) and \(\vB\) with our expression for \(\va\) to get the following:
    \begin{align*} \va \cdot \vT \amp= (c_1 \vT + c_2 \vN +c_3 \vB)\cdot \vT \\ \amp= c_1 (\vT \cdot \vT) + c_2 (\vN \cdot \vT) +c_3 (\vB \cdot \vT) \\ \amp=c_1 \\ \va \cdot \vN \amp= (c_1 \vT + c_2 \vN +c_3 \vB)\cdot \vN \\ \amp= c_1 (\vT \cdot \vN) + c_2 (\vN \cdot \vN) +c_3 (\vB \cdot \vN) \\ \amp=c_2 \\ \va \cdot \vB \amp= (c_1 \vT + c_2 \vN +c_3 \vB)\cdot \vB \\ \amp= c_1 (\vT \cdot \vB) + c_2 (\vN \cdot \vB) +c_3 (\vB \cdot \vB) \\ \amp=c_3 \end{align*}
    Because we our TNB frame directions are all orthogonal to each other and unit vectors, we can compute the amount of acceleration in each of these directions with a dot product!

    Definition 10.6.5.

    The tangential component of acceleration is defined as \(a_{\vT} = \va \cdot \vT \) and the normal component of acceleration is defined as \(a_{\vN} = \va \cdot \vN \text{.}\) Because \(\vT\) and \(\vN\) are orthogonal to each other and unit vectors, \(a_{\vT}\) and \(a_{\vN}\) will give the amount of acceleration in the direction of travel and the direction of turning, respectively.
    You may notice that we only gave names for the accelerations in the tangential and normal directions above. It turns out that the acceleration vector for motion along a curve can only have components in the tangential and normal directions to the curve. It is not possible for the acceleration vector of parameterized curve to have any component in another direction (like \(\vB\)).

    Key Idea 10.6.6.

    If \(\va(t)\) is the acceleration vector for a parameterization of a curve in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \va=a_{\vT} \vT + a_{\vN} \vN \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    We claim that the scalar \(a_{\vN}\) measures the acceleration due to change in the direction of \(\vv\) and the scalar \(a_{\vT}\) measures the acceleration due to the change in magnitude of \(\vv\text{.}\) This interpretation of \(a_{\vT}\) and \(a_{\vN}\) follows from a neat product rule argument based on how we split the velocity vector into speed (magnitude) and \(\vT\) (direction). We start by splitting velocity into its direction and magnitude.
    \begin{equation*} \vv(t) = \text{speed}(t) \enspace \vT(t) \end{equation*}
    Taking the derivative of the velocity using a product rule, we get the following for the acceleration.
    \begin{equation*} \va(t) = \frac{d\vv}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(\text{speed}) \enspace \vT + (\text{speed}) \enspace \frac{d}{dt}(\vT) \end{equation*}
    We can rearrange the terms in our definition for \(\vN\) to solve for \(\vT'(t)\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \vN=\frac{\vT'}{\Vert\vT'\Vert} \Rightarrow \vT'= \Vert\vT'\Vert \vN \end{equation*}
    Applying this to our acceleration equation above, we get
    \begin{equation} \va(t) = \frac{d\vv}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(\text{speed}) \enspace \vT + (\text{speed}) \enspace \left\Vert{\frac{d\vT}{dt}}\right\Vert \vN \tag{10.6.1} \end{equation}
    This algebraic splitting of acceleration gives a nice way to interpret \(a_{\vT}\text{.}\) Specifically, \(a_{\vT}\) is the rate of change of speed!
    The scalar \(a_{\vN}\) is harder to interpret right now because our algebraic splitting shows that \(a_{\vN} = (\text{speed}) \Vert\frac{d\vT}{dt}\Vert\text{.}\) Because \(\frac{d\vT}{dt}\) and \(\Vert\frac{d\vT}{dt}\Vert\) are just as hard to calculate as \(\vN\) directly (which is awful as shown in Activity10.4.4), we do not have a great interpretation for the meaning of \(a_{\vN}\text{.}\) We will return to the meaning of \(a_{\vN}\) later in this section, but for now you should think of \(a_{\vN}\) as the acceleration due to a change in the direction of the velocity vector. In other words, \(a_{\vN}\) is the amount of acceleration due to turning.
    Note that nothing in the argument for Key Idea10.6.6 depended on being in three dimensions and all of our calculus tools used work for any \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\)

    Definition 10.6.7.

    When we refer to the splitting of the acceleration (vector), we mean that we want to explicitly describe how much of the acceleration is in the unit tangent direction and how much is in the unit normal direction (relative to the motion on the curve).
    In particular, we want to find \(a_{\vT}\) and \(a_{\vN}\) such that \(\va = a_{\vT} \vT + a_{\vN} \vN\text{.}\) Figure10.6.8 shows graphically what this splitting looks like. You can use the slider at the top of the interact to change the location on the curve where the splitting of the acceleration is shown. You should look at different points along the curve to see how each of \(\vT\text{,}\) \(\vN\text{,}\) \(a_{\vT}\text{,}\) and \(a_{\vN}\) change at different locations. The vector \(a_{\vN} \vN\) is drawn twice to demonstrate its relationship to \(\vN\) and how \(\va\) is a vector sum of \(a_{\vT} \vT\) and \(a_{\vN} \vN\text{.}\)

    Example 10.6.9.

    (a)
    In this example we will look at the splitting of acceleration for an algebraically simple curve parameterized by \(\vr(t)=\langle t, t^2,t^3\rangle\) as \(t\) goes from 0 to 2. We will first look at calculating the splitting of acceleration for all \(t\)-values in our interval. In particular, we will calculate \(\vv\text{,}\) \(\va\text{,}\) \(\vT\text{,}\) \(a_{\vT}\text{,}\) \(a_{\vN}\text{,}\) and \(\vN\) as functions of \(t\text{.}\) The calculation of the velocity and acceleration are generally speaking the most familiar.
    \begin{align*} \vr(t) \amp=\langle t, t^2,t^3\rangle \\ \vv(t) \amp=\langle 1, 2t,3t^2\rangle \\ \va(t) \amp=\langle 0, 2,6t\rangle \end{align*}
    As this example will show, it is possible to state each of our other calculations in terms of velocity and acceleration.
    The next quantity to calculate is \(\vT\text{.}\) Note that \(\text{speed}(t)=\Vert\vv(t)\Vert= \sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}\) for our example.
    \begin{equation*} \vT(t) = \frac{\vv}{\Vert\vv\Vert} = \frac{\langle 1, 2t,3t^2\rangle}{\sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}} \end{equation*}
    This will allow us to calculate \(a_{\vT}\) next from the definition \(a_{\vT} = \va \cdot \vT\)
    \begin{align*} a_{\vT} = \va \cdot \vT \amp= \langle 0, 2,6t\rangle \cdot \frac{\langle 1, 2t,3t^2\rangle}{\sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}} \\ \amp= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}} \left(1(0)+2(2t)+6t(3t^2)\right)\\ \amp= \frac{4t+18t^3}{\sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}} \end{align*}
    So far in this example, we have used the definitions to calculate both \(\vT\) and \(a_{\vT}\text{,}\) but \(\vN\) is quite difficult to calculate using the definition. In fact, we gave up trying to use the defintion when calculating \(\vN\) in Activity10.4.4.
    Our next couple of steps will use some vector calculations related to Definition10.6.7. Because the unit tangent and unit normal vectors are both length one and are orthogonal to each other, then \(\Vert \va \Vert^2 =(a_{\vT})^2+(a_{\vN})^2\text{.}\) This equation comes from a Pythagorean Theorem argument as shown by the right triangle of vectors in Figure10.6.10
    In particular, we can solve for \(a_{\vN}= \sqrt{\Vert \va \Vert^2 -(a_{\vT})^2}\) which will not require an interpretation of positive/negative because \(a_{\vN}\) will always be non-negative. For our example,
    \begin{align*} a_{\vN} \amp= \sqrt{\Vert \va \Vert^2 -(a_{\vT})^2} \\ \amp= \sqrt{(4+36t^2)- \left(\frac{4t+18t^3}{\sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}}\right)^2}\\ \amp= \sqrt{(4+36t^2)- \frac{(4t+18t^3)^2}{1+4t^2+9t^4}}\\ \amp= \sqrt{\frac{(1+4t^2+9t^4)(4+36t^2)-(4t+18t^3)^2}{1+4t^2+9t^4}}\\ \amp= \sqrt{\frac{4(9t^4+9t^2+1)}{1+4t^2+9t^4}} \end{align*}
    Note how we were able to find the acceleration in the normal direction (\(a_{\vN} \vN\)) by subtracting out the acceleration in the tangential direction. Our calculation above utilizes that \(a_{\vN}\) is the length of this acceleration in the normal direction. The other side to this is that we can calculate \(\vN\) as a function of \(t\) without having to go through the difficulties involved in \(\frac{d\vT}{dt}\text{.}\) Specifically, \(a_{\vN} \vN = \va - a_{\vT} \vT\) implies that \(\vN = \frac{\va - a_{\vT} \vT}{a_{\vN}}\text{.}\) This is hugely useful because we were able to calculate everything on the right side of this equation in terms of \(\vv\) and \(\va\text{.}\) For our example,
    \begin{align*} a_{\vN} \vN \amp= \va - a_{\vT} \vT \\ \amp= \langle 0, 2,6t \rangle - \frac{4t+18t^3}{\sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}} \frac{\langle 1, 2t,3t^2\rangle}{\sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}} \end{align*}
    Thus we can write \(\vN\) as
    \begin{align*} \vN \amp= \frac{\va - a_{\vT} \vT}{a_{\vN}}\\ \amp= \sqrt{\frac{1+4t^2+9t^4}{4(9t^4+9t^2+1)}}\left(\langle 0, 2,6t \rangle - \frac{4t+18t^3}{\sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}} \frac{\langle 1, 2t,3t^2\rangle}{\sqrt{1+4t^2+9t^4}}\right) \end{align*}
    While this process may seem a bit algebraically tedious, these steps are much less difficult than finding \(\vN\) directly (as in Activity10.4.4) AND we get insight into how the acceleration is split in the direction of travel and direction of turning. Graphically, we can represent this splitting with the following figure.
    (b)
    In many circumstances, we need to compute the splitting of acceleration for only one parameter value. For this example, we will look at how much cleaner the computations above will be if we want to look only at \(t=1\text{.}\) The first steps for calculating \(\vv\) and \(\va\) will be the same as before.
    \begin{align*} \vr(t) \amp=\langle t, t^2,t^3\rangle \\ \vv(t) \amp=\langle 1, 2t,3t^2\rangle \\ \va(t) \amp=\langle 0, 2,6t\rangle \end{align*}
    You may want to go back and note that this was the only calculus done in the example above; all other steps were vector calculations and properties of orthogonal or unit vectors. We will follow the same method as above, but instead of applying these ideas for all \(t\)-values, we will look only at \(t=1\text{.}\) Remember if the formula we are using works for all \(t\text{,}\) then the formula will work for a specific \(t\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \vr(1) \amp=\langle 1, 1, 1 \rangle \\ \vv(1) \amp=\langle 1, 2, 4 \rangle \\ \va(1) \amp=\langle 0, 2, 6 \rangle \end{align*}
    The next quantity to calculate is \(\vT\text{.}\) Note that \(\text{speed}(1)=\Vert\vv(1)\Vert= \sqrt{1+4+9}=\sqrt{14}\) for our example.
    \begin{equation*} \vT(1) = \frac{\vv}{\Vert\vv\Vert} = \frac{\langle 1, 2, 3 \rangle}{\sqrt{14}} \end{equation*}
    This will allow us to calculate \(a_{\vT}(1)\) next from the definition \(a_{\vT}(1) = \va(1) \cdot \vT(1)\)
    \begin{align*} a_{\vT}(1) = \va(1) \cdot \vT(1) \amp= \langle 0, 2, 6 \rangle \cdot \frac{\langle 1, 2 , 3 \rangle}{\sqrt{14}} \\ \amp= \frac{1}{\sqrt{14}} \left(1(0)+2(2)+3(6)\right)= \frac{22}{\sqrt{14}} \end{align*}
    We now use \(a_{\vN}(1) = \sqrt{\Vert \va(1) \Vert^2 -(a_{\vT}(1))^2}\) to efficiently calculate the normal component of the acceleration.
    \begin{align*} a_{\vN}(1) \amp= \sqrt{\Vert \va(1) \Vert^2 -(a_{\vT}(1))^2} \\ \amp= \sqrt{(40)- \left(\frac{22}{\sqrt{14}}\right)^2}\\ \amp= \sqrt{\frac{560}{14}-\frac{22^2}{14}}\\ \amp= \sqrt{\frac{76}{14}} \end{align*}
    We can now compute how much acceleration there is due to turning and use that explicitly compute \(\vN(1)\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} a_{\vN}(1) \vN(1) \amp= \va(1) - a_{\vT}(1) \vT(1) \\ \amp= \langle 0, 2,6 \rangle - \frac{22}{\sqrt{14}} \frac{\langle 1, 2, 3\rangle}{\sqrt{14}}\\ \amp= \langle -\frac{11}{7},-\frac{16}{7},\frac{9}{7} \rangle \end{align*}
    You can show that the length of \(\langle -\frac{11}{7},-\frac{16}{7},\frac{9}{7}\rangle\) is the same as our calculation for \(a_{\vN}\) above. In particular, this means that
    \begin{equation*} \vN = \frac{\va - a_{\vT} \vT}{a_{\vN}} = \sqrt{\frac{7}{38}} \left\langle -\frac{11}{7},-\frac{16}{7},\frac{9}{7} \right\rangle \end{equation*}
    With some diligent computations, you should be able to verify that this result is the same as applying \(t=1\) to our expression for \(\vN\) from the first task.
    The method used in the previous example can be applied broadly and shows how to calculate all parts of Definition10.6.7 in terms of \(\vv\) and \(\va\) (for both an interval or a single value of \(t\)).

    Efficient Calculation of \(a_{\vT}, a_{\vN}, \vT, \vN\) in terms of \(\vv\) and \(\va\).

    \begin{align*} \vT \amp= \frac{\vv}{\Vert\vv\Vert} \\ a_{\vT} \amp= \frac{\va\cdot\vv}{\Vert\vv\Vert} \Rightarrow a_{\vT} \vT = \frac{\va\cdot \vv}{\vv \cdot \vv} \vv\\ a_{\vN} \amp= \Vert \va - a_{\vT} \vT \Vert = \sqrt{\Vert \va \Vert - (a_{\vT})^2}= \sqrt{(\va \cdot \va)- \frac{(\va\cdot\vv)^2}{\Vert\vv\Vert}} \\ a_{\vN} \vN \amp= \va - a_{\vT} \vT \\ \vN \amp= \frac{\va - a_{\vT} \vT}{a_{\vN}} \end{align*}

    Exercise10.6.4.4, Exercise10.6.4.5, and Exercise10.6.4.6 offer great conceptual and physical meaning to the different measurements involved in the splitting of acceleration. We will take a moment now to relate \(a_{\vN}\text{,}\) the acceleration due to a change in the direction of travel, and \(\kappa\text{,}\) the curvature, which measures rate of change for the direction of travel on a curve.
    The previous statement may seem a bit like we said the same thing twice, but \(a_{\vN}\) and \(\kappa\) are not the same. Theorem10.5.6 shows that
    \begin{equation*} \kappa = \frac{\left\Vert \frac{d\vT}{dt} \right\Vert}{ \left\Vert \vr'(t) \right\Vert} \end{equation*}
    We can solve for \(\left\Vert \frac{d\vT}{dt} \right\Vert\) in terms of \(\kappa\) to get \(\left\Vert \frac{d\vT}{dt} \right\Vert= \kappa (speed)\text{.}\)
    We saw earlier in this section that
    \begin{equation*} a_{\vN}= (speed) \Vert\frac{d\vT}{dt}\Vert \end{equation*}
    Thus, we can write \(a_{\vN}\) in terms of speed and curvature as
    \begin{equation} a_{\vN}=\kappa (\text{speed})^2 \tag{10.6.2} \end{equation}
    Equation (10.6.2) should help to demonstrate the difference between \(a_{\vN}\) and \(\kappa\text{.}\) Specifically, \(\kappa\) measures how fast the path is curving and \(a_{\vN}\text{,}\) the normal component of acceleration, measures the acceleration felt due to turning. Equation (10.6.2) shows that if you and I are moving along the same path but you are going twice as fast as I am, then you will feel four times as much acceleration due to turning (even though we are going along the exact same path). Curvature is how fast the path is turning and \(a_{\vN}\) is how fast you feel like you are turning (based on your speed/the parameterization).

    Subsection 10.6.3 Summary

    • The acceleration vector for a parameterization of a curve, \(C\text{,}\) can be split into parts in the direction of travel and direction of turning
      \begin{equation*} \va=a_{\vT}\vT+a_{\vN}\vN \end{equation*}
      where \(a_{\vT}=\va \cdot \vT\) and \(a_{\vN}=\va \cdot \vN\text{.}\)
    • The scalar \(a_{\vT}\) measures the rate of change of the speed. The scalar \(a_{\vN}\) measure the acceleration due to change in direction and can be calculated by
      \begin{equation*} a_{\vN}=\kappa (speed)^2 \end{equation*}
      where \(\kappa\) is the curvature.
