    Section 10.5 Curvature

    Motivating Questions

    • How can you measure how fast a path is turning (regardless of the parameterization)?
    • What is the radius of curvature for a path at a given location?

    Subsection 10.5.1 Introduction

    In Section10.4, we defined \(\vT\) and \(\vN\) to measure the direction of travel and the direction of turning for an oriented curve in space. Both \(\vT\) and \(\vN\) are unit vectors that are used to capture direction aspects of motion. In the next couple of sections, we will look at a few different ways to measure the magnitude of various aspects of our motion and turning on a curve in space.
    Note here that given a parameterization of a curve in space, the speed will give us an idea about how fast we are moving along the curve, but we dont have a way to measure how much turning is happening at a given location. In this section, we will measure how quickly a curve is turning. How quickly a curve is turning is a different measurement from how fast an object is turning as it moves along a path. We will return to the idea of how fast an object is turning as it moves along a path in Section10.6.

    Preview Activity 10.5.1.

    As Chief Engineer and CEO at Steer Clear, you have done great work in transforming information from your location tracking system (LTS) into information on the position, velocity, and distance traveled by your car. In order to make the self-driving part of the self-driving car company, you will need to compare information from the LTS (which describes how your car is moving) to GPS location data for the network of roads (which describe the paths your car should be taking). Since everyone on the road drives differently, we would like to measure how quickly a stretch of road is turning in different places. This measurement should not depend on the particular driver but should be a property of the path the road takes. Remember that your car cannot look forward in time to see how it will need to turn, but the car can look forward along the road to see how the path changes.

    (b)

    Our preview activity showed how looking at the change in \(\vT\text{,}\) the direction of travel, for small steps (in terms of arclength) along our curve will allow us to measure the rate at which a curve is turning. Note that this will be different than measuring how quickly an object traveling along the path needs to turn in order to stay on the path. The arguments in the preview activity do not depend on how an object is traveling along the path, only on the path itself.

    Subsection 10.5.2 Curvature

    We claim that \(\vr_1(t)\text{,}\) the unit speed parameterization of a curve is unique; If you and I drive the same path and both maintain a speed of exactly 1 meter per second, then for any point on the path, we will both reach that point in the same amount of time. For instance, it will take us both exactly 15 seconds to reach the point that is 15 meters down the path (remember that the parameter value for \(\vr_1\) is the same as the distance traveled along the curve). Conceptually, you can see how using the unit speed parameterization will allow us to measure properties of the curve and avoid questions about how different ways of moving along the path will affect our measurements.
    We will measure how quickly a path turns by examining how quickly the direction of travel changes in terms of the unit speed parameterization. We will apply the a classic calculus approach to this measurment in order to have a precise measuremet. If \(\vr_1(t)\) is the unit speed parameterization of our curve \(C\) and we look a section of our path from \(\vr(t_0)\) to \(\vr(t_0+\Delta h)\text{,}\) then we will first look at how different the direction of travel is over this segment of length \(\Delta H\text{.}\) A plot a sample curve and two points separated by an arclength of \(\Delta h\) is shown in Figure10.5.3.
    We want to know how fast the direction of travel is changing at a particular point (say \(\vr(t_0)\)), so we will approximate the change in the direction of travel on an interval around this point and take the limit as the size of the interval goes to zero. In order to compute the difference between the directions of travel at the beginning and end of our interval, we will put both \(\vT(t_0+\Delta h)\) and \(\vT(t_0)\) starting at the same location, then we can use vector subtraction to find \(\vT(t_0+\Delta h)-\vT(t_0)\text{,}\) the change in the direction of travel over the given interval. This vector subtraction is shown using the triangle of vectors on the left of Figure10.5.4.
    So for a step of size \(\Delta h\) in arclength along our curve, we have a change in direction of \(\vT(t_0+\Delta h)-\vT(t_0)\) in the direction of travel. In order to approximate the rate of change for the direction of travel, we will need to look at the ratio
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\vT(t_0+\Delta h)-\vT(t_0)}{\Delta h} \end{equation*}
    which is shown as the scaled red vector at the bottom of Figure10.5.4.
    The unit normal vector, \(\vN\text{,}\) already measures the direction of turning (the direction in which the direction of travel is changing). We want to measure as a scalar how quickly the direction of turning is changing along our path, so we take the magnitude of this difference quotient as our approximation of rate of change for the direction of travel over our interval.
    \begin{equation*} \left\Vert \frac{\vT(t_0+\Delta h)-\vT(t_0)}{\Delta h} \right\Vert \end{equation*}
    This completes step one of our classic calculus approach. As we take smaller step sizes in our parameter (arclength), we can see that our approximation will give a better approximation of how fast the direction of travel is changing along our path at the point \(\vr(t_0)\text{.}\)
    If we take the limit as \(\Delta h \rightarrow 0\text{,}\) then we have step three of our classic calculus approach to measure how quickly a curve is turning at the point \(P=\vr_1(t_0)\text{,}\) which can be calculated by
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{\Delta h \rightarrow 0} \left\Vert \frac{\vT(t_0+\Delta h)-\vT(t_0)}{\Delta h} \right\Vert = \left\Vert \frac{d\vT}{ds} \right\Vert \end{equation*}
    Remember that the parameterization used in every part of the above argument is the unit speed parameterization which is why are able to take the derivative of \(\vT\) with respect to the arclength.

    Definition 10.5.5.

    If \(C\) is a smooth space curve and \(s\) is the parameter for the unit speed parameterization of \(C\text{,}\) then the curvature, \(\kappa\text{,}\) of \(C\) is
    \begin{equation*} \kappa = \kappa(s) = \vecmag{ \frac{d \vT}{ds} } \end{equation*}
    Remember that the parameter for the unit speed parameterization \(C\) is the arclength traveled along the curve. Note that curvature is denoted with \(\kappa\text{,}\) the Greek lowercase letter kappa.

    Activity 10.5.2.

    In this activity we will use our previous work in finding unit speed parameterizations of lines and circles to make sense of the definition of curvature.
    (a)
    Recall that in Example10.3.6 we found that the unit speed parameterization of a line through the points \((1,2,3)\) and \((-2,3,1)\) to be \(\vr_1(t)= \langle 1,2,3\rangle + \frac{t}{\sqrt{14}} \langle -3,1,-2 \rangle\text{.}\) The generalization of this example would be that the unit speed parameterization of a line through \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\) with direction vector \(\langle a,b,c\rangle\) can be given by
    \begin{equation*} \vr(s)=\langle x(s),y(s),z(s) \rangle = \langle x_0+\frac{a}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}s , y_0+\frac{b}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}s ,z_0+ \frac{c}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}s\rangle \end{equation*}
    Compute the speed of this parameterization to verify that this has unit speed. This will also show that \(\vr'(s)=\vv(s)=\vT(s)\) (which is not true in general).
    (b)
    Calculate \(\vT(s)\text{,}\) \(\frac{d\vT}{ds}\text{,}\) and \(\left\Vert \frac{d\vT}{ds} \right\Vert\) for the unit speed parameterization of a line.
    (c)
    Write a few sentences to explain why your result for the calculation of \(\kappa=\left\Vert \frac{d\vT}{ds} \right\Vert\) of a line makes sense.
    (d)
    Recall that in Activity10.3.2 we found that a constant speed parameterization of circle in 2-space of radius \(R\) centered at the origin. Modifying this parameterization by multiplying the parameter by \(\frac{1}{\text{speed}}\) will give the following parameterization.
    \begin{equation*} \vr(s) = \left\langle R \cos\left(\frac{s}{R}\right), R \sin\left(\frac{s}{R}\right)\right\rangle \end{equation*}
    Compute the speed of this parameterization to verify that this has unit speed. This will also show that \(\vr'(s)=\vv(s)=\vT(s)\) (which is not true in general).
    (e)
    Calculate \(\vT(s)\text{,}\) \(\frac{d\vT}{ds}\text{,}\) and \(\left\Vert \frac{d\vT}{ds} \right\Vert\) for the unit speed parameterization of a circle centered at the origin.
    (f)
    Write a few sentences to explain why your result for the calculation of \(\kappa=\left\Vert \frac{d\vT}{ds} \right\Vert\) of circle will be constant. You should also address why circles with larger radii will have have smaller curvature.
    Notice that a unit speed parameterization will have the property that \(\vr'(s)=\vv(s)=\vT(s)\) which is not true in general, but rather the calculation and interpretation of \(\vT\) is as simple as possible.
    The definition of curvature relies on our ability to parameterize curves with unit speed. Since finding unit speed parametrization is very difficult to compute directly, we would like to be able to calculate the curvature of a curve in terms of any parametrization \(\vr(t)\text{.}\)

    Theorem 10.5.6.

    If \(\vr\) is a parameterization of a curve \(C\text{,}\) and if \(\vr'(t)\neq \vzero\) and \(\vr''(t)\) exists, then the curvature \(\kappa\) of \(C\) can be calculated by
    \begin{equation*} \kappa(t) = \frac{\left\Vert \vT'(t) \right\Vert}{ \left\Vert \vr'(t) \right\Vert} \end{equation*}
    Further, we can efficiently calculate curvature in terms of the velocity and acceleration functions by
    \begin{equation} \kappa = \frac{\Vert \vr'(t) \times \vr''(t) \Vert}{\Vert \vr'(t) \Vert^3}= \frac{\Vert \vv(t) \times \va(t) \Vert}{\Vert \vv(t) \Vert^3}\tag{10.5.1} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    We will give the outline of a proof of the first result here in order to emphasize that this result comes from a chain rule argument related to the change of parameters going to a unit speed parameterization.
    Let \(\vr(t)\) be some parameterization of our curve \(C\text{.}\) Remember that in order to calculate the curvature, \(\vT\) will need to have an input corresponding to the parameter of the unit speed parameterization, which means the input of the \(\vT\) will need to measure the arclength of \(C\) to the point of interest. We will use a function \(s(t)\) with input being the parameter of \(\vr(t)\) and the output of \(s(t)\) will be the arclength on \(C\) to the point \(\vr(t)\text{.}\) Note that \(s(t)\) is computed using Theorem10.3.5 and that the derivative \(\frac{ds}{dt}\) will be \(\Vert\vr'(t)\Vert\text{.}\)
    We can write \(\vT\) as a function of the parameter \(t\) by the composition \(\vT(t)=\vT(s(t))\text{.}\) Taking the derivative of this composition with respect to \(t\) will utilize a chain rule argument.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d\vT}{dt}=\frac{d}{dt}\left(\vT(s(t))\right)=\frac{d\vT}{ds} \frac{ds}{dt} \end{equation*}
    Note here that \(\frac{d\vT}{dt}=\vT'\) is the same derivative of \(\vT\) we have seen a few times.
    Because \(\frac{ds}{dt}\) is a scalar valued function we can divide both sides of the equation by it to get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\vT'}{\frac{ds}{dt}}=\frac{d\vT}{ds} \end{equation*}
    Using our idea above from Theorem10.3.5, we can substitute \(\frac{ds}{dt}= \Vert\vr'(t)\Vert\) and take the magnitude of each side to get the following
    \begin{equation} \left\Vert \frac{\vT'}{\Vert\vr'(t)\Vert} \right\Vert = \left\Vert\frac{d\vT}{ds} \right\Vert =\kappa\tag{10.5.2} \end{equation}
    If this chain rule argument seems a little technical to you, there is a nice conceptual interpretation of the equation above. Specifically, if we want to convert a measurement like curvature or the derivative of \(\vT\) between the unit speed parameterization and any other parameterization, we literally need to adjust by the speed. Equation (10.5.2) shows that the adjustment between the derivative of \(\vT\) with respect to the unit speed parameter and another parameter is to divide by the variable speed.
    We omit the proof of (10.5.1) because the argument involves a lot of technical algebraic ideas that do not offer a lot of insight into what is measured by curvature. Exercise10.5.4.6 will guide you through this calculation if you are interested in these steps.
    Before we do our examples and activities that calculate curvature for other kinds of paths, we will take a moment to use (10.5.1) to make sense of when curvature will not be defined or make sense. From an algebraic perspective, the curvature will not be defined when the speed is zero. This should make sense by the fact that if you are not moving along the curve (speed is zero), then it does not make sense to measure how quickly the curve is turning. In Section10.6, we will make sense of why \(\Vert \vv(t) \times \va(t) \Vert\) being zero means that there will be zero curvature, which is very different than the curvature being undefined.

    Example 10.5.7.

    In this example, we will calculate the curvature of the parabola given by \(y=x^2\) for \(-2 \lt x \lt 2\text{.}\) We will parameterize this curve in three dimensions so that we can use (10.5.1) (cross product is defined in 3D only). The vector-valued function \(\vr(t)=\langle t,t^2,0\rangle\) with \(t\in[-2,2]\) will parameterize the curve given by \(y=x^2\) for \(-2 \lt x \lt 2\text{.}\)
    Using this parameterization, we get the following set of results:
    \begin{align*} \vr'(t)=\vv(t) \amp=\langle1,2t,0\rangle \\ \vr''(t)=\va(t) \amp=\langle0,2,0\rangle \\ \vv(t)\times\va(t) \amp=\langle0,0,2\rangle \\ \Vert\vv(t)\times\va(t)\Vert \amp=2 \\ \Vert\vv(t)\Vert \amp=\sqrt{1+4t^2} \end{align*}
    Using (10.5.1), we get the curvature to be
    \begin{equation*} \kappa(t) = \frac{\Vert \vv(t) \times \va(t) \Vert}{\Vert \vv(t) \Vert^3} = \frac{2}{(1+4t^2)^{3/2}} \end{equation*}
    Comparing the points \((0,0)\) and \((-\frac{3}{2},\frac{9}{4})\) on Figure10.5.8, you can see that the parabola will have a larger curvature at \((0,0)\) because the path is bending more at \((0,0)\) than at \((-\frac{3}{2},\frac{9}{4})\text{.}\) Using our result above, we can see that the curvature at \((0,0)\)(when \(t=0\)) is \(\kappa(0)=2\) and the curvature at \((-\frac{3}{2},\frac{9}{4})\) (when \(t=-\frac{3}{2}\)) is \(\kappa(-\frac{3}{2})=\frac{2}{(10)^{3/2}}\approx0.06325\text{.}\) This calculation of curvature matches with our visual description from above.
    We can also think of the curvature as a function of the parameter being used (in our case \(t=x\)). A plot of \(\kappa(t) = \frac{2}{(1+4t^2)^{3/2}}\) as a function of \(t=x\) is shown in Figure10.5.9. You can see that the curvature is highest near the vertex of the parabola and the curvature gets closer to zero as you look farther up the parabola. This should make sense because the parabola is flatter away from the origin.
    Suppose at the instant \(t=x=0\) you were driving along the path given by \(y=x^2\) at unit speed and your steering wheel locked up preventing you from changing how you were turning. Because the steering wheel is held fixed, you will no longer stay on the path given by \(y=x^2\) but instead will drive in a circle. What do you think the radius of the circle will be?
    It turns out that the radius of this circle will be \(\frac{1}{\kappa(0)}=\frac{1}{2}\text{!}\) This shouldnt be too surprising because in Activity10.5.2, we saw that the curvature of a circle of radius \(R\) is \(\frac{1}{R}\text{.}\) In general, the radius of curvature is defined to be \(\frac{1}{\kappa}\) and the circle that is tangent to the curve and has radius \(\frac{1}{\kappa}\) is called the osculating circle. The osculating circle can be described as the circle that best approximates the curve at the tangent point since the osculating circle goes through the tangent point and has the same curvature as the original path.
    In Figure10.5.10, you can see a plot of the path \(y=x^2\) with the osculating circle drawn at a particular point. You can use the slider to change the parameter value at which the osculating circle is drawn. You should notice how the radius of the circle becomes much larger as you move farther away from the vertex in either direction. Remember that curvature and the radius of curvature have an reciprocal relationship; large curvature means small radius and small curvature means a large radius.

    Subsection 10.5.3 Summary

    • We define the curvature \(\kappa\) of a curve in 2- or 3-space to be the rate of change of the magnitude of the unit tangent vector with respect to arc length, or
      \begin{equation*} \kappa = \left\Vert \frac{d\vT}{ds} \right\Vert \end{equation*}
    • Curvature can be efficiently calculated by the following formula for curves in space:
      \begin{equation*} \kappa = \frac{\Vert \vr'(t) \times \vr''(t) \Vert}{\Vert \vr'(t) \Vert^3}= \frac{\Vert \vv(t) \times \va(t) \Vert}{\Vert \vv(t) \Vert^3} \end{equation*}
    • The radius of curvature is \(\frac{1}{\kappa}\) and describes the radius of a circle corresponding to how quickly a path is turning.
