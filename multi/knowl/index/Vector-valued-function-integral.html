Integrating a Vector-Valued Function.

If \(\vr(t) = x(t) \vi + y(t) \vj + z(t) \vk\) with \(t\in[a,b]\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} \int^b_a \vr(t) \ dt = \left(\int^b_a x(t) \ dt\right) \vi + \left( \int^b_a y(t) \ dt \right) \vj + \left(\int^b_a z(t) \ dt\right) \vk\text{,} \end{equation*}
provided that all three of the integrals on the right-hand side exist. An antiderivative of a vector-valued function \(\vr\) is a vector-valued function \(\vR\) such that
\begin{equation*} \vR\, '(t) = \vr(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
The indefinite integral \(\int \vr(t) \ dt\) of a vector-valued function \(\vr\) is the most general antiderivative of \(\vr\) and represents the family of all antiderivatives of \(\vr\text{.}\) The indefinite integral of \(\vr(t)\) is denoted by
\begin{equation*} \int \vr \ dt = \int \langle x(t),y(t),z(t) \rangle \ dt = \left\langle \int x(t) \ dt, \int y(t) \ dt,\int z(t) \ dt\right\rangle\text{.} \end{equation*}
